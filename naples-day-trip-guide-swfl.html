Naples Day Trip: Art, Beaches & Bites Worth the Drive

Why Naples Deserves a Full Day on Your SWFL Itinerary

Just about an hour south of Fort Myers along U.S. 41 or I-75, Naples punches well above its weight as a day-trip destination. It's one of those rare Florida cities where pristine white-sand beaches sit within easy walking distance of a genuinely charming downtown packed with galleries, boutiques, and some of the best restaurants in all of Southwest Florida. Whether you're a snowbird looking for a change of scenery, a tourist on a longer SWFL stay, or a newcomer sizing up the region, Naples is well worth the drive — and having your own set of wheels makes all the difference.

Morning: Start at the Naples Pier & Lowdermilk Park

The iconic Naples Pier is the city's most recognizable landmark and the perfect place to kick off your morning. Arrive early (parking fills up fast, especially in season) and stroll the 1,000-foot pier jutting out over the Gulf. Pelicans perch on the railings, dolphins cruise by with surprising regularity, and on a clear morning the view back toward the shoreline is simply stunning.

From there, head a mile or so north to Lowdermilk Park, a beloved local beach park with restrooms, volleyball nets, picnic pavilions, and easy public parking. It's a little less crowded than the pier area and a great spot to spread out a towel and ease into the day.

Midday: Fifth Avenue South — Browse, Sip & Explore

Naples' downtown core, centered on Fifth Avenue South, is one of the most walkable and visually delightful streets in all of Florida. Drive in, park the car, and spend a couple of hours on foot. You'll find:

  • Art galleries galore — Naples has one of the highest concentrations of fine-art galleries per capita in the country. Even if you're just browsing, many are open to walk-ins and worth a peek.
  • Boutique shopping — From local jewelry designers to high-end home décor, Fifth Avenue has a surprisingly eclectic retail mix that beats any mall.
  • Outdoor café culture — Grab an iced coffee or a light lunch at one of the many sidewalk cafés. The people-watching alone is worth the stop.

Third Street South is another worthwhile detour just a few blocks away — a quieter, tree-shaded alternative with its own cluster of restaurants and specialty shops.

Afternoon: Naples Botanical Garden & the Baker Museum

After lunch, cool off in a couple of Naples' most impressive cultural attractions. The Naples Botanical Garden spans 170 acres and features stunning themed gardens representing ecosystems from Florida, the Caribbean, Asia, and Brazil. It's surprisingly immersive — plan at least 90 minutes. Tickets can be purchased in advance online, which is strongly recommended during the busy winter season.

Art lovers shouldn't miss Artis—Naples and its on-campus Baker Museum, one of the finest art museums in the Southeast. The permanent collection spans modern and contemporary works, and visiting exhibitions rotate throughout the year. The campus itself is architecturally gorgeous and just a short drive from downtown.

Evening: Sunset Dinner & the Drive Home

Naples sunsets over the Gulf are legitimately world-class — golden, wide, and unhurried. Head back to the Naples Pier or find a spot along the beach around 7–8 p.m. (depending on the season) and watch the sky do its thing. You've earned it.

For dinner, Naples has no shortage of excellent options. A few local favorites worth seeking out:

  • Bayside Seafood Grill & Bar — Waterfront setting, fresh Gulf seafood, reliably excellent.
  • The French on Fifth Avenue — A cozy, upscale bistro perfect for a slower, celebratory evening meal.
  • Stanno's Pizzeria — Casual, beloved by locals, and a great choice if you're feeding a hungry family after a full day out.

The drive back north to Cape Coral or Fort Myers is smooth and scenic at night — a relaxed close to a genuinely great day. That kind of freedom is exactly what SWFL exploring is all about.
