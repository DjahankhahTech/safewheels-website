Venice, Florida: Shark Teeth, Historic Charm & Gulf Sunsets

Why Venice Deserves a Spot on Your SWFL Itinerary

Tucked along the Gulf Coast between Fort Myers and Sarasota, the small city of Venice is one of Southwest Florida's most rewarding day-trip destinations — and one of its most overlooked. Unlike the better-known stretches of beach to the south, Venice moves at a gentler pace. Its streets are lined with Mediterranean Revival architecture, its shores hide ancient fossilized shark teeth, and its sunsets rank among the prettiest on Florida's west coast. Whether you're a first-time visitor flying into RSW or PGD, a snowbird looking for a fresh adventure, or a new resident still discovering the region, Venice is well worth the roughly 60-to-75-minute drive north from the Cape Coral / Fort Myers area.

The best part? A comfortable, fuel-efficient rental car makes the whole trip effortless. The World's "Shark Tooth Capital" — Caspersen Beach

Venice proudly calls itself the Shark Tooth Capital of the World, and for good reason. Millions of fossilized shark teeth — some dating back 10 million years — wash ashore here regularly, eroded from ancient offshore deposits. The best hunting ground is Caspersen Beach, the southernmost and most natural of Venice's public beaches. Unlike the groomed, heavily trafficked shores you'll find elsewhere, Caspersen is wonderfully wild: a long, narrow strip backed by Australian pines, with dark shell-and-fossil-flecked sand that rewards patient eyes.

  • What to bring: A fine-mesh sand sifter (sold at local shops) makes hunting much easier. Polarized sunglasses help you spot dark teeth against the sand.
  • Best time to go: Low tide in the morning, especially after a storm, when fresh fossils are exposed.
  • What to look for: Tiny black or dark-gray triangles, anywhere from a few millimeters to a few centimeters long. Mako, bull shark, and even giant megalodon teeth have been found here.
  • Parking tip: There's a free lot at Caspersen Beach Park — arrive early on weekends as it fills up fast.

Even if you don't find a single tooth (though you almost certainly will), the beach itself is beautiful and blissfully uncrowded compared to its neighbors.

Strolling Historic Downtown Venice

After the beach, head a few minutes north into Historic Downtown Venice — a genuinely walkable, postcard-pretty grid of streets centered on Venice Avenue and Tampa Avenue. The city was master-planned in the 1920s by the Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers with a distinctly Italian aesthetic, and that Mediterranean Revival architecture is still remarkably intact. You'll find arched colonnades, terracotta roof tiles, and fountain plazas that make it feel more like a European piazza than a Florida beach town.

Spend an hour or two browsing the boutiques, galleries, and antique shops that line the avenues. Grab a coffee at one of the sidewalk cafés and watch the world go by at a very civilized pace. The Venice Museum & Archives on Tampa Avenue is a small but fascinating stop if you want to understand how this unique city came to be.

Downtown Venice is also a wonderful place to pick up a local souvenir — shark tooth jewelry is everywhere, and much of it is crafted by local artisans rather than mass-produced.

Lunch, Bites & Local Flavors

Venice punches well above its weight when it comes to dining. Here are a few locally loved spots to consider:

  • Sharky's on the Pier — A Venice institution. Sit on the outdoor deck overlooking the Gulf, order the fresh grouper sandwich, and let the sea breeze do the rest. The Venice Fishing Pier right next door is free to walk and worth a stroll.
  • The Crow Bar — A laid-back craft beer and comfort food spot downtown, popular with locals and a great place to recharge mid-day.
  • Snook Haven — A short drive east along the Myakka River, this rustic fish camp is a hidden gem: open-air dining, cold drinks, live music on weekends, and an "Old Florida" atmosphere that feels completely authentic.
  • Gelato & Espresso Bar downtown — Perfect for an afternoon pick-me-up before sunset. Several gelato shops dot Venice Avenue and the quality is consistently excellent.

Sunset at Venice Beach & the Jetties

Time your departure from downtown to arrive at Venice Beach or the South Jetty about 45 minutes before sundown, and you'll be rewarded with one of the finest sunset views in all of Southwest Florida. The South Jetty at the Intracoastal Waterway entrance is a particularly magical spot — you can watch dolphins surfing the channel current while the sky turns shades of pink and orange over the Gulf. Bring a light layer; the breeze picks up as the sun dips, and it's wonderfully refreshing after a full day in the heat.
