Sea Turtle Nesting Season: A Guide to SWFL's Most Magical Beaches

Why Sea Turtle Nesting Season Is SWFL's Best-Kept Secret

Every year from May through October, something quietly extraordinary unfolds along the Gulf Coast of Southwest Florida. Female loggerhead, green, and leatherback sea turtles — some weighing over 300 pounds — haul themselves ashore under the cover of darkness to dig nests and lay their eggs in the same beaches where they were born decades earlier. It's one of nature's most awe-inspiring rituals, and the good news is that some of the highest-density nesting beaches in the entire United States are right here in your backyard.

Most visitors don't even realize it's happening. That's what makes nesting season such a special secret for those in the know — and such a rewarding reason to venture up and down the coastline rather than sticking to one spot. Whether you're a snowbird spending the summer, a nature-loving tourist, or a newcomer still exploring the region, building a turtle-themed beach road trip is one of the most memorable things you can do in SWFL.

The Best Beaches to Spot Nests (and Hatchlings)

You won't see turtles nesting by just wandering onto any beach — location and timing matter. Here are the standout spots worth the drive:

  • Lovers Key State Park (Fort Myers Beach area): One of the most beloved stretches of undeveloped shoreline in Lee County, Lovers Key sees consistent loggerhead activity each season. The park's natural, low-light environment makes it ideal for nesting. Early morning walks often reveal fresh nest markings staked out by conservation volunteers.
  • Barefoot Beach Preserve (Bonita Springs): Consistently one of the top nesting beaches in Lee County, Barefoot Beach is monitored daily by trained volunteers from the Conservancy of Southwest Florida. Stakes and tape marking active nests are a common sight, and the preserve's strict no-lighting and no-disturbance rules make it a genuine sanctuary.
  • Delnor-Wiggins Pass State Park (Naples): Just a scenic drive south, this park offers some of the most pristine nesting habitat in Collier County. Rangers and volunteers actively monitor nests, and late-summer evenings sometimes bring the incredible spectacle of a full boil — dozens of tiny hatchlings scrambling toward the sea at once.
  • Cape Romano Shoals & Marco Island beaches: Further south, these remote shores are serious nesting territory. Access requires some planning, but the reward is a near-wilderness experience along one of Florida's wildest coastlines.
  • North Captiva & Cayo Costa (accessible by ferry): For those willing to make a day adventure of it, these barrier islands — reachable via ferry from Pine Island — offer nesting habitat almost entirely free of development and artificial light.

How to Watch Responsibly — The Rules That Protect the Turtles

Sea turtles are federally protected under the Endangered Species Act, and for good reason — their populations are still recovering from centuries of exploitation. Responsible viewing isn't just good ethics; it's the law. Here's what every beach visitor should know during nesting season:

  • Never use white flashlights or phone flashlights on the beach at night. Artificial light disorients nesting females and causes hatchlings to crawl toward roads instead of the ocean. Use a red-filtered light only if necessary.
  • Stay well back from any turtle you spot. If a female feels threatened while crawling ashore, she will abort her nesting attempt and return to the sea — potentially losing an entire clutch of eggs. Give her at least 30 feet of space and stay behind her, never in front.
  • Never disturb, sit on, or remove nest stakes and tape. Volunteer monitors stake nests to protect them from foot traffic. These markers should never be touched.
  • Fill in holes and flatten sand castles before you leave. Depressions in the sand can trap disoriented hatchlings heading toward the water.
  • Pack out all trash, especially fishing line and balloons. Sea turtles mistake these for jellyfish and can choke or die from ingesting them.

Many beaches offer organized, ranger-led turtle walks during peak season — Delnor-Wiggins and Barefoot Beach both run programs that let you observe a nesting female or hatchling release under expert guidance. These fill up fast, so book well in advance through the Florida State Parks website.

Planning Your Sea Turtle Road Trip: Timing & Logistics

The nesting season runs from roughly May 1 through October 31, with peak nest laying in June and July and peak hatching in August and September. If witnessing a hatchling release is your goal, late August through late September is your sweet spot.

The ideal turtle road trip covers a loose arc from Fort Myers Beach south to Naples, with optional extensions north toward Manasota Key or south toward Marco Island. Here's a suggested one-day coastal loop:

  • Morning: Arrive at Lovers Key State Park at first light (6–7 a.m.) and walk the beach to spot fresh nest markings from the previous night's activity. Pack a picnic and enjoy the park's kayak trails afterward.
  • Midday: Drive south along Bonita Beach Road to Barefoot Beach Preserve. Chat with the morning volunteer monitors — they're incredibly knowledgeable and love sharing what they've seen. A cold lunch at one of the beachside spots on Bonita Beach Road sets you up for the afternoon.
  • Afternoon: Continue south on US-41 to Delnor-Wiggins Pass State Park in Naples. If you've pre-registered for an evening ranger turtle walk, this is your base. Otherwise, enjoy a swim and watch the sunset from one of SWFL's most unspoiled barrier island beaches.
  • Evening: If you're not on a ranger walk, head back north along the Tamiami Trail and stop for seafood — the stretch between Naples and Bonita Springs has no shortage of excellent waterfront options.

The whole loop is comfortably doable in a day, covering about 60–80 miles of some of the most scenic coastal driving in Florida. Local Conservation Groups Worth Supporting

Sea turtle conservation in SWFL runs almost entirely on the energy of passionate local volunteers. If this experience moves you — and it will — consider supporting the organizations doing the daily work:

  • Conservancy of Southwest Florida — monitors and protects nests across Lee and Collier counties, runs educational programs, and leads sea turtle rehabilitation efforts at their Naples wildlife clinic.
  • SCCF (Sanibel-Captiva Conservation Foundation) — monitors nesting on Sanibel and Captiva Islands and runs one of the longest-running sea turtle research programs in the state.
  • Rookery Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve — protects critical nesting habitat south of Naples and offers fantastic educational resources for visitors.

Even sharing photos responsibly on social media — no flash, no close-ups that reveal exact nest locations — helps raise awareness without putting turtles at risk. These ancient creatures have been navigating these same shores for over 100 million years. With a little care from the rest of us, they'll keep returning for millions more.
